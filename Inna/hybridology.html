Hybridology

Human-animal composites were part of a menagerie of wonders within the European culture since Antiquity. In medieval texts, monster hybrids were representations of collective fears, desires, and sins - a mix of religion, politics, pseudoscience, and magic. A monster is a hybrid phenomenon. It represents a category crisis that threatens the integrity of classification - the subject matter of Hybridology. This work treats mythological hybrid body as a set of data and is a taxonomic classification system. Representations of hybrids were reconstructed in 3D graphic software. As in the reconstruction of extinct species, the imagery was treated as a set of morphological characters (for example, absence/presence of tail, genitals, scales) and aspects of behavior based on medieval bestiary literature. The hybrid bodies were then compared statistically, resulting in a tree-like structure (cladogram) that shows heredity relations between various hybrids. The Tree of Morphology therefore represents a non-linear view of hybrid evolution (that of a branching bush) and contrasts with the more canonical iconography - linear "Chain of Being" or “March of Progress”.
Another layer in Hybridology is a reconstruction of mythological hybrid embryos in various stages of development. Biologists observed in the 19th century that early-stage embryos of animals (as different as human and fish) are nearly indistinguishable from one another. To Darwin, this was evidence for common origin. The fact that an early human embryo possesses gills and tail (subsequently lost) suggests that humans descended from animals with gills and tails. In Hybridology, the same logic is applied to mythological hybrids. Their embryos are reconstructed and regressed to an early stage, where they converge into a form common to all of them, an embryonic archetype.
Like a mythical, monstrous hybrid in search of a boundary between reality and fable, Hybridology seeks interfaces between science, pseudo-science, and art.
